## LT 4.4 — Emotional Development & Relationships

**LT Name:** Emotional Development & Relationships
**LT ID:** LT 4.4
**Competency:** 4 — Consent, Safety & Healthy Relationships
**Knowledge Type:** T2 Horizontal with embedded T1 Know layer (biological basis of emotional experience)
**Assessment Route:** Reasoning-quality rubric. Primary tasks: scenario analysis, case study analysis using a described or fictional relationship, or structured written reflection. Students must be explicitly offered the option to use a fictional example — personal disclosure must never be required. The T1 content layer (biological accuracy) is assessed for correctness within the T2 analytical task.

**Within-Competency Prerequisites:**
- **LT 4.1 (Bodies, Boundaries & Consent)** — Conceptual accelerator. Consent and boundary vocabulary enriches relational pattern analysis in LT 4.4 but does not logically gate it.
- **LT 4.2 (Puberty, Health & Safe Choices)** — Strong soft enabler. Hormonal and developmental biological content covered in LT 4.2 serves as the primary T1 Know scaffold for LT 4.4 Bands C–D. Cross-referenced rather than re-taught when LT 4.2 precedes LT 4.4.

**LT Definition:** I can use knowledge of emotional and relational development to identify, explain, and analyse patterns in emotional experiences and relationships.

## KUD Table

### Band A — Water + Air Dragons (5–7)

**Know:**
1. Feelings have names — common examples include happy, sad, angry, scared, proud, ashamed, excited, and surprised.
2. People can have different feelings about the same situation; neither person's feeling is wrong.

**Understand:** My feelings give me information about what I care about and what I need.

**Do:** I can name my feelings and describe how I want to be treated by my friends.

**Progression lever:** Starting point — establishes feeling vocabulary and first-person relational awareness as the foundation for all later bands.

**Disciplinary warrant:** Gottman, J., & DeClaire, J. (1997). *Raising an Emotionally Intelligent Child*. Simon & Schuster.

---

### Band B — Earth Dragons (8–10)

**Know:**
1. Caring friendships have observable and repeatable qualities: they include listening, kindness, fairness, and showing up when the other person is upset.
2. A friendship that consistently makes someone feel bad, scared, or ashamed is not a healthy one.
3. Feelings within a relationship can change over time — both within the same relationship and from one relationship to another.

**Understand:** The way a friendship makes me feel over time, not just on one bad day, tells me something important about whether it is good for me.

**Do:** I can describe what a caring friendship looks like and explain how my feelings change when a friendship is going well or not going well.

**Progression lever:** Scope (A→B): single internal state → recurring relational patterns. Reasoning: naming feelings → explaining how feelings connect to relational quality over time.

**Disciplinary warrant:** Bukowski, W. M., Hoza, B., & Boivin, M. (1994). Measuring friendship quality during pre- and early adolescence. *Journal of Social and Personal Relationships*, 11(3), 471–484.

---

### Band C — Fire Dragons (10–12)

**Know:**
1. Sex hormones released during puberty — including oestrogen and testosterone — affect the intensity of emotional reactions, which is why feelings can feel stronger or more confusing during early adolescence. *(Cross-reference LT 4.2 Band C for fuller biological content on puberty development.)*
2. A pattern in a relationship is a repeated tendency across multiple situations, not a single event; caring patterns include consistent mutual respect, and uncaring patterns include dismissal, control, or persistent disregard for the other person's feelings.
3. Emotional intensity during puberty is a normal developmental phase, not a permanent personality trait or evidence that something is wrong.

**Understand:** When my emotions feel unusually intense or confusing, this is often my developing body, not a sign that something is permanently wrong with me or with my relationships.

**Do:** I can describe how physical and emotional changes during puberty affect my feelings and relationships, and identify patterns that distinguish a caring relationship from an uncaring one.

**Progression lever:** Complexity (B→C): simple caring vs. uncaring → puberty-informed understanding of why emotions shift in relational contexts. Precision: single descriptions → identifying repeating patterns across multiple situations.

**Disciplinary warrant:** Steinberg, L. (2014). *Age of Opportunity: Lessons from the New Science of Adolescence*. Houghton Mifflin Harcourt.

### Band D — Metal + Light Dragons (12–13)

**Know:**
1. The prefrontal cortex — responsible for emotional regulation and impulse control — is not fully mature until the mid-twenties; the amygdala is fully developed earlier, which explains why adolescent emotional responses can be fast and intense before conscious reflection catches up. *(Cross-reference LT 4.2 Band D for hormonal and puberty-specific biological content.)*
2. Attraction is associated with elevated dopamine and norepinephrine activity; rejection activates neural pathways that overlap with physical pain; loss triggers a non-linear grief response involving multiple emotional states.
3. Healthy relational patterns include reciprocity, honest communication, and respect for boundaries; unhealthy patterns include attempts to control, emotional manipulation, deliberate inconsistency designed to create dependency, and consistent dismissal of the other person's feelings.
4. The course teaches three theoretical frameworks for understanding emotional systems, and these function as the analytical toolkit for Band D work: (i) **dual-process models** of emotion and cognition — fast, intuitive, automatic processing alongside slower, deliberative, reflective processing, and how the two interact; (ii) the **stress-response window** — how arousal level shapes access to analytical thinking, and what falls outside a person's window of tolerance; (iii) **habit formation** — cue–routine–reward loops and how repeated emotional responses become patterned over time.
5. Claims about emotional experience come in different kinds, and good analysis distinguishes them. (i) **Empirical neuroscientific claims** — such as prefrontal cortex maturation timelines — are supported by converging evidence and held with high confidence. (ii) **Interpretive claims** — such as "rejection activates neural pathways that overlap with physical pain" — make reasonable inferences from more limited evidence and should be held more provisionally. (iii) **Normative claims** — such as what counts as a "healthy" relational pattern — reflect values and cultural context, not biological necessity. At Band D, students identify which kind of claim they are making or evaluating when they analyse a scenario.

**Understand:** My emotional experiences, including attraction, rejection, and loss, have biological and psychological explanations — understanding those explanations does not make the experience less real, but it does give me more agency in choosing how to respond.

**Do:** I can explain the biological and psychological basis of an emotional experience, identify healthy and unhealthy patterns in a described relationship, and use course concepts to analyse a personal or fictional experience of attraction, rejection, or loss.

**Progression lever:** Transfer (C→D): own direct experience → described or fictional scenarios requiring conceptual application. Reasoning: identify patterns → explain and analyse using biological, psychological, and course-concept frameworks. Precision: caring/uncaring binary → healthy/unhealthy with explanatory depth.

**Disciplinary warrant:** Siegel, D. J. (2013). *Brainstorm: The Power and Purpose of the Teenage Brain*. Jeremy P. Tarcher/Penguin.

### Band E (G9–10)

**Know:**
1. Social learning theory holds that relationship scripts — learned patterns for how relationships are supposed to work — are absorbed from family, peer culture, and media and are often treated as natural rather than learned.
2. Power in a relationship is not always visible; in peer relationships it shows up in specific observable patterns — who changes their plans when the other asks, who apologises more often regardless of who was actually wrong, whose emotional state sets the tone for the interaction, whose interests are talked about more often, whose social reputation is protected at the other's expense, and who would bear the greater cost if the relationship ended.
3. The same behaviour can carry different meanings depending on the relational history, the power differential involved, and the cultural context in which the relationship exists.

**Understand:** Every relational dynamic has multiple perspectives — what feels normal or acceptable from inside a relationship may look very different from outside it, or from the other person's vantage point.

**Do:** I can evaluate how biological, psychological, and social factors interact to shape a relational pattern, comparing the perspectives of the people involved and explaining how power or context affects the dynamic.

**Progression lever:** Reasoning (D→E): explain + apply concepts → evaluate multi-factor interactions. Complexity: single emotional experience → relational dynamic shaped by intersecting factors. Scope: single perspective → multiple perspectives including power analysis.

**Disciplinary warrant:** Felmlee, D., & Sprecher, S. (2000). Close relationships and social psychology: Intersections and future paths. *Social Psychology Quarterly*, 63(4), 365–376.

---

### Band F (G11–12)

**Know:**
1. Attachment theory (Bowlby; Ainsworth; Mikulincer & Shaver) describes patterns — commonly named secure, anxious-preoccupied, dismissive-avoidant, and disorganised — that develop in early childhood and shape later relational tendencies. The theory has a **strong empirical core** (the basic secure/insecure distinction and its early-childhood origins are well-evidenced), a **contested middle** (the stability and exact structure of the four-style taxonomy into adulthood is debated in the research literature), and a **popularised periphery** (social-media and self-help "attachment style" discourse routinely outruns the underlying evidence). At Band F, attachment theory is used both as an analytical framework for examining relationships and as a body of claims to evaluate critically against the evidence base.
2. Cultural scripts for relationships vary substantially across cultures, generations, and social contexts; what is considered healthy, normal, or appropriate in a relationship reflects cultural and historical power, not biological necessity.
3. Relational repair — following conflict, harm, or breakdown — involves acknowledgement, accountability, and negotiated restoration of trust; it is a skill that can be developed rather than an innate capacity.

**Understand:** The frameworks I use to analyse a relationship are not neutral — each illuminates certain aspects and obscures others; choosing a framework is itself an analytical and ethical act.

**Do:** I can critically evaluate complex relational dynamics using multiple analytical frameworks, explaining how biological, psychological, social, and cultural factors intersect, and constructing a justified position on how the dynamic might be navigated or improved.

**Progression lever:** Reasoning (E→F): evaluate → critically evaluate using multiple explicit frameworks. Complexity: intersecting biological/psychological/social factors → adding the cultural and historical layer. Transfer: analyse a given situation → construct a justified recommendation that can be defended against alternative positions.

**Disciplinary warrant:** Mikulincer, M., & Shaver, P. R. (2007). *Attachment in Adulthood: Structure, Dynamics, and Change*. Guilford Press. Kimmel, M. (2000). *The Gendered Society*. Oxford University Press (for the cultural scripts content at Band F Know item 2).

## Authoring Notes (v2)

### Formative sequencing at Band D

The Band D Do statement integrates three cognitively distinct operations: explaining biological basis, identifying relational patterns, and applying course concepts to analyse a specific experience. Before the summative integrated task, teachers should assess each component formatively in sequence — (i) a biological explanation task on the scope of Know items 1 and 2; (ii) a pattern identification task using a clearly fictional scenario against Know item 3; (iii) a reflective analysis task using course concepts from Know item 4, including the epistemic-status distinction from Know item 5. This sequencing resolves the cognitive load concern raised in panel review.

### Retrieval practice flagging at Band D

Three Band D Know items contain content that is conceptually unfamiliar to most 12–13 year olds and requires deliberate retrieval practice and spaced re-exposure: prefrontal cortex / amygdala maturation timelines (item 1); dopamine, norepinephrine, and pain-overlap circuitry (item 2); and the three course frameworks named in item 4. A single exposure is insufficient. Retrieval practice opportunities — low-stakes recall tasks, spaced revision, mini-quizzes — should be built into lessons before the summative integrated task.

### Cross-reference to LT 8.1 at Bands E–F

Students at Bands E and F are at precisely the age where they consume high volumes of relationship content online, much of it epistemically low-quality. The relational pattern analysis developed in LT 4.4 and the information verification skills developed in LT 8.1 (Information Verification & Media Literacy) are mutually reinforcing. Teachers delivering either LT at Band E or F should cross-reference the other: pattern analysis is stronger when paired with scrutiny of the source; source scrutiny is more meaningful when the student has a framework for what the content is claiming. This is a scheme-of-work note, not a content revision.

### Band D Know item 4 — course concept confirmation

Know item 4 names three specific course concepts as the analytical toolkit: dual-process models of emotion and cognition, the stress-response window, and habit formation. If the programme's current scope and sequence uses different or additional frameworks at this level (for example Elephant/Rider from the AoA curriculum, cognitive schemas, attachment-style sketches as preparation for Band F), the Know cell should be updated to match what is actually taught. The three named here are defensible defaults drawn from the programme purpose and prior authoring notes.

### Competency 4 design commissions arising from panel review

Two competency-level gaps surfaced during panel review that cannot be resolved within LT 4.4 and require separate design work at the Competency 4 level:

**T3 companion LT for dispositional emotional self-management.** LT 4.4 is a T2 analytical LT. It correctly assesses the student's capacity to reason about emotional and relational experience. It does not and cannot assess dispositional enactment — whether the student regulates their own mood and navigates their own relationships well in practice. Mood regulation is named in the programme purpose for Competency 4, and Claxton's panel review flagged its absence from assessable content as a structural gap. A T3 companion LT is required to close this gap. Design responsibility sits with the Competency 4 team.

**Emotional activation safeguarding protocol.** Scenario tasks involving attraction, rejection, and loss can activate genuine emotion in the student completing them, even when the scenario is explicitly fictional. Teachers require a protocol for recognising when a student has shifted from analytical engagement to personal activation and for responding appropriately. This is a safeguarding document at competency or framework level, not an LT 4.4 artefact. The protocol should be produced in coordination with the school's safeguarding lead and the Circle Solutions programme.

### Prerequisite sequencing decision

LT 4.2 is designated a strong soft enabler rather than a hard prerequisite because a teacher could in principle teach the Band C–D biological content within LT 4.4 instruction. In practice, given programme design, LT 4.2 should precede LT 4.4, and the Know cells at Bands C–D should cross-reference rather than re-teach.

### Three items deferred to criterion bank brief

Three panel concerns have been identified as correctly resolved at the rubric / criterion-bank level rather than at the KUD level. These are carried forward into the LT 4.4 criterion generation brief:

- **Wiliam — Band D formative sub-criteria.** Band D criteria split the three-component Do into at least three distinct criteria (biological explanation accuracy, relational pattern identification, conceptual application using course frameworks) with descriptors at each competency level.
- **Christodoulou — Band F framework set.** Band F criteria explicitly name the framework set (attachment theory, dual-process models, social learning theory, power/context analysis); Competent requires meaningful use of at least two frameworks; Extending requires three or more plus evaluation of framework choice itself.
- **Wineburg — "justified position" definition.** Band F criteria operationalise justification on four dimensions: evidentiary fit, acknowledgement of alternative explanations, transparency about framework choice, and acknowledgement of limits. Each appears as a rubric descriptor element.
