# **REAL School Wellbeing Framework — KUD Chart (Know \+ Understand \+ Do)**

*Version 1 | 20 April 2026 | Working draft authored from programme purpose*

---

## **About this document**

This document pairs the REAL School wellbeing framework's fourteen learning targets across bands A-D with three knowledge layers:

* **Know** — specific factual content students need to hold. Concentrated in Competency 6; present in Competencies 3 and 4 where specific facts matter; explicitly absent in Competencies 1, 2, 5, and 7 because their factual grounding sits in Competency 6\.  
* **Understand** — transferable ideas students should grasp conceptually, as distinct from what they perform. Authored from programme purpose and grounded in named disciplines.  
* **Do** — existing band statements and observation indicators from the current framework, unchanged.

The Understand and Know layers are authored from the programme's purpose. They are not borrowed from external curricula. External frameworks will be used for cross-reference audit in a later pass — to examine convergence and justify divergence, not to source content.

Each Understand is a transferable idea: a conceptual grasp a student carries into new situations, not a restatement of the performance. Each Know is a specific factual item a student needs to hold. Progression across bands uses the six progression levers (independence, complexity, scope, precision, reasoning, transfer) rather than topic escalation.

This is a working draft. Expected next steps: teacher review, disciplinary audit, cross-reference against external frameworks, and integration decisions about whether the KUD layers fold into band statements or remain as parallel reference.

---

## **Knowledge architecture — how the framework is structured**

The framework distributes Know content deliberately rather than symmetrically across competencies:

* **Competency 6 (Wellbeing Science & Literacy)** is the formal home for Type 1 factual knowledge. It carries the neuroscience of stress, emotion, attention, and habit, plus the frameworks for evaluating health information. Know is most dense here.  
* **Competency 3 (Physical Wellbeing & Self-Care)** carries a light Know layer at Bands A-B (basic body-needs facts) that transitions to integration with C6 from Band B onward.  
* **Competency 4 (Consent, Safety & Healthy Relationships)** carries Know content at Bands B-D where specific factual items matter (anatomical terminology, puberty changes, health facts about STIs/pregnancy). Band A has one factual item (body-part names).  
* **Competencies 1, 2, 5, 7** have no standalone Know layer. Their factual grounding is in Competency 6\. This is a deliberate design choice: treating these competencies as analytical/dispositional (Understand \+ Do) rather than factual means the framework doesn't force artificial Know content where the programme's actual epistemology places factual grounding elsewhere.

This structure is more honest than imposing a full Know layer on every competency. It also reflects the knowledge-contingency relationships: the dispositional competencies (C1, C3 partial, C5 partial, C7) are knowledge-contingent on C6, and their Knows live there.

## **Competency 1 — Emotional Intelligence**

**Competency Definition:** The ability to understand, express, and manage one's emotions while empathetically responding to others.

**Competency-level Understand:** My emotions and others' emotions are physiological and psychological patterns I can learn to notice, make sense of, and respond to — they are information, not verdicts on who I am.

**Know layer:** None standalone. Factual grounding lives in Competency 6 (LT 6.1 — Brain, Body & Wellbeing Science). LT 1.1 is knowledge-contingent on LT 6.1.

### **LT 1.1 — Self-Awareness & Regulation**

**Knowledge Type:** Type 3 — Dispositional **Assessment Route:** Multi-informant observation; Reflection 360; not summatively graded **Prerequisites:** LT 6.1 (knowledge-contingent)

| Band | Do — Observation Indicator (existing) | Understand (new) | Progression lever | Disciplinary warrant |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| A | I can notice what my body is telling me, name a feeling, and use one calming strategy to return to learning. | My feelings have names, and naming them helps me notice them. | Baseline | Affect labelling research (Lieberman) |
| B | I can identify a common personal trigger and choose a practised strategy to manage my response. | What I feel is often set off by something specific, and I can learn what sets me off. | Complexity: feelings named → feelings linked to causes | Emotion regulation (Gross) |
| C | I can apply a chosen strategy in a challenging situation and reflect on how well it worked. | A strategy either works for me or doesn't depending on the situation, and I can tell by checking afterwards. | Reasoning: pattern-naming → strategy evaluation; Transfer: practised contexts → challenging contexts | Self-regulated learning (Zimmerman) |
| D | I can apply my strategies under stress across different settings, explain what worked and what didn't, and state one change for next time. | How I regulate depends on what's available to me in a given context, and adjusting my approach across settings is how regulation gets more reliable. | Scope: single setting → multiple settings; Reasoning: evaluation → context-sensitive adjustment | Contextual emotion regulation (Aldao) |

**Knowledge Type:** Type 3 — Dispositional **Assessment Route:** Multi-informant observation; Reflection 360; not summatively graded **Prerequisites:** LT 1.1 (developmentally — self-awareness of emotion precedes accurate reading of others' emotion)

## **Competency 2 — Attention & Reflective Practices**

**Competency Definition:** The ability to develop present attention and reflection for clarity and wise action.

**Competency-level Understand:** Attention is a trainable capacity with specific mechanisms, and the quality of my decisions depends on the quality of the reflection I bring to them.

**Know layer:** None standalone. Factual grounding for attention mechanisms lives in Competency 6\.

### **LT 2.1 — Focused Attention & Strategy**

**Knowledge Type:** Type 2 — Horizontal **Assessment Route:** Reasoning-quality rubric; reflective tasks applied to real situations **Prerequisites:** None

| Band | Do (existing) | Understand (new) | Progression lever | Disciplinary warrant |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| A | I can use my senses to attend to one task for a short time and describe how I brought my attention back when it wandered. | My attention wanders, and I can bring it back. | Baseline | Attention development (Posner) |
| B | I can use a practised attention strategy during a task and describe what made it easier or harder to focus. | Attention is affected by what's going on around me and inside me, and some conditions help more than others. | Complexity: attention wanders → attention has identifiable supports and disruptors | Attention research (Rueda); executive function development |
| C | I can evaluate how an attention strategy affected my focus during a specific task and explain why it did or didn't help. | Strategies work through specific mechanisms, and knowing the mechanism helps me choose the right strategy for the situation. | Reasoning: observation → mechanism; Precision: general supports → specific strategy-effects | Metacognitive monitoring (Flavell) |
| D | I can sustain attention during a challenging task, identify what is pulling it away, and use a deliberate strategy to return. | The things that pull my attention away are often more informative about what I need than about the task itself. | Reasoning: mechanism-awareness → diagnostic use; Transfer: chosen strategies → in-context adjustment | Mindfulness research (caveats per MYRIAD); self-regulation in challenge |

**Knowledge Type:** Type 2 — Horizontal **Assessment Route:** Reasoning-quality rubric; novel scenario tasks; written analysis **Prerequisites:** None

| Band | Do (existing) | Understand (new) | Progression lever | Disciplinary warrant |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |

## **Competency 3 — Physical Wellbeing & Self-Care**

**Competency Definition:** The ability to understand and apply health-enhancing practices and routines that build resilience.

**Competency-level Understand:** My physical wellbeing is built through habits I can design, maintain, and rebuild when they break — it is not a state I possess but a practice I sustain.

**Knowledge-contingency note:** Both LTs depend on LT 6.1 (habit loop, brain-body mechanisms) for their deeper Understands. The Band A layer is approachable without this grounding; B, C, and D increasingly depend on it.

### **LT 3.1 — Health Literacy & Habits**

**Knowledge Type:** Type 2 — Horizontal **Assessment Route:** Reasoning-quality rubric; habit design and evaluation tasks; Strive data as evidence within reasoning tasks **Prerequisites:** LT 6.1 from Band B onward

| Band | Know (new) | Understand (new) | Do (existing) | Progression lever | Disciplinary warrant |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| A | Basic body needs: sleep, food, water, movement, hygiene. | My body needs specific things to feel well. | I can name my body's basic needs and practise simple personal hygiene routines with reminders. | Baseline | Basic health literacy |
| B | Relationships between sleep/food/movement and daily feeling-states (standalone Know folds into C6 LT 6.1 from this band). | How I feel day-to-day is shaped by what I do consistently, not just once. | I can explain how sleep, food, and movement affect how I feel, and set a goal for one healthy habit. | Reasoning: needs → consistency; Transfer: momentary needs → patterned practice | Behavioural health (Glanz); habit research (Wood) |
| C | Know content for this band is in LT 6.1 (habit loop mechanism, neuroplasticity). | Habits break down for specific reasons, and noticing the reason is the first step in adjusting the habit. | I can track one habit for a week, identify a barrier, and explain why I want to keep or change it. | Complexity: pattern → pattern with barriers; Reasoning: observation → causal analysis | Behaviour change (Fogg); self-monitoring literature |
| D | Know content for this band is in LT 6.1 (system-level neuroscience) and LT 6.2 (evidence evaluation). | Designing for my life means planning for the real conditions I live in, including the ones that disrupt me, rather than an idealised version of myself. | I can compare two sources of health information, create a balanced weekly habits plan, and adjust it based on logged results. | Scope: single habit → balanced plan; Reasoning: reactive adjustment → anticipatory design | Implementation intentions (Gollwitzer); design thinking applied to health |

**Knowledge Type:** Type 3 — Dispositional **Assessment Route:** Multi-informant observation; Strive longitudinal data as process evidence; Reflection 360; not summatively graded **Prerequisites:** LT 3.1 (analytical capability); LT 6.1 (foundational knowledge)

| Band | Know (new) | Understand (new) | Do — Observation Indicator (existing) | Progression lever | Disciplinary warrant |

## **Competency 4 — Consent, Safety & Healthy Relationships**

**Competency Definition:** The ability to understand bodies, boundaries, consent, puberty, and safe, respectful choices; accessing trustworthy health information.

**Competency-level Understand:** Respect for bodies and boundaries — my own and others' — is the foundation of safety in every kind of relationship, and consent is a skill I can learn to give, seek, and recognise.

### **LT 4.1 — Bodies, Boundaries & Consent**

**Knowledge Type:** Type 2 — Horizontal **Assessment Route:** Reasoning-quality rubric; novel scenario tasks; structured roleplay. Specialist delivery recommended from Band C. **Prerequisites:** None directly; draws on LT 1.2 (empathy) and LT 5.1 (communication)

| Band | Know (new) | Understand (new) | Do (existing) | Progression lever | Disciplinary warrant |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |

## **Competency 6 — Wellbeing Science & Literacy**

**Competency Definition:** The ability to understand how the brain and body produce the experiences of stress, emotion, habit, and attention — and to evaluate health information using this knowledge.

**Competency-level Understand:** My inner experiences of stress, emotion, habit, and attention are produced by specific mechanisms in the brain and body, and the quality of health information depends on the quality of evidence behind it.

*Knowledge-foundation note: Competency 6 is the formal home for Type 1 knowledge in the framework. Its Knows underwrite the knowledge-contingent dispositions in Competencies 1 (LT 1.1) and 3 (LT 3.1, LT 3.2), and its LT 6.2 is prerequisite to LT 4.2.*

### **LT 6.1 — Brain, Body & Wellbeing Science**

**Knowledge Type:** Type 1 — Hierarchical **Assessment Route:** Rubric with clear criteria; structured explanation tasks; vocabulary checks **Prerequisites:** None (foundational)

| Band | Know (new) | Understand (new) | Do (existing) | Progression lever | Disciplinary warrant |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| A | Names for basic body signals (heart beating fast, tight tummy, tiredness); what sleep and food do for the body and brain. | My body gives me information about how I feel before I think about it. | I can name body signals that tell me something about how I feel, and describe what sleep and food do for my body and brain. | Baseline | Interoception research (Craig) |
| B | The fight/flight/freeze response and what happens in the body; the three parts of the habit loop (cue, routine, reward). | My body's stress response happens automatically to protect me, and my habits run as a repeated loop. | I can explain what happens in my body during a fight, flight, or freeze response, and describe the three parts of the habit loop. | Complexity: one mechanism → two mechanisms | Stress physiology (Sapolsky); habit research (Wood) |
| C | The amygdala and prefrontal cortex and their functions during stress; the concept of neuroplasticity; the habit loop at mechanism level. | Specific brain structures have specific functions, and my brain changes in response to what I practise. | I can explain how the amygdala and prefrontal cortex interact during a stressful moment, explain what neuroplasticity means, and describe how the habit loop explains behaviour change at the level of mechanism. | Precision: general mechanism → structure-specific mechanism; Transfer: mechanism → applies to my own change | Neuroanatomy of emotion (LeDoux); neuroplasticity (Doidge) |

## **Competency 7 — Metacognitive Self-Direction**

**Competency Definition:** The ability to notice, analyse, and intentionally adjust one's own thinking and learning patterns.

**Competency-level Understand:** My thinking and behaviour run on patterns I can notice and influence — metacognition is the practice of standing outside my own mind long enough to see what it's doing.

**Know layer:** None standalone. Metacognition is a practice, not a factual domain. Factual grounding for why patterns form (brain, habit) lives in Competency 6\.

*Cross-competency note: Competency 7 is transferable across all other competencies. Its Understands intentionally connect to, not duplicate, the metacognitive work in Competencies 1, 2, and 3\.*

### **LT 7.1 — Pattern Analysis & Adjustment**

**Knowledge Type:** Type 2 — Horizontal **Assessment Route:** Reasoning-quality rubric; structured reflective tasks; portfolio entries applied to real situations. Transfer is the test. **Prerequisites:** None directly; draws on LT 2.2 (reflective decision-making) and LT 1.1 (self-awareness)

| Band | Do (existing) | Understand (new) | Progression lever | Disciplinary warrant |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| A | I can describe one thing I noticed about how I reacted to a situation and say what I might do differently next time. | I can think about how I did something, not just what I did. | Baseline | Metacognition development (Flavell) |
| B | I can identify a pattern in how I tend to respond in a type of situation and give a reason for why that might happen. | Some of what I do isn't random — I tend to do similar things in similar situations, and that's useful information about me. | Complexity: single instance → pattern across instances | Self-knowledge research (Wilson) |
| C | I can analyse a pattern across more than one situation, explain what drives it, and describe a specific adjustment I have made or will make. | Patterns have drivers, and identifying the driver is what makes adjusting possible — noticing alone doesn't change much. | Reasoning: pattern-noticing → causal analysis; Transfer: observation → intentional change | Self-regulated learning (Zimmerman); metacognitive regulation (Schraw) |
| D | I can evaluate a pattern in my thinking or responses across different contexts, justify what sustains it, and describe the effect of a specific adjustment I have made. | What sustains a pattern is often different from what started it, and adjustments need to address the sustaining conditions to hold over time. | Reasoning: causal → sustaining-conditions analysis; Scope: one context → multiple contexts; Transfer: single adjustment → evaluated adjustment | Habit change research (Wood); metacognitive strategy use (Hattie) |

**Knowledge Type:** Type 3 — Dispositional **Assessment Route:** Multi-informant observation; student self-reflection; Reflection 360 developmental conversation **Prerequisites:** LT 7.1 (knowledge-contingent — students need the T2 analytical capability before this disposition can be meaningfully observed)
