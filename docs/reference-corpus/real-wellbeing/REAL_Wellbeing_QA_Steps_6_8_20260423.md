### Full commentary for flagged LTs

#### LT 4.2 Puberty, Health & Safe Choices — mean 86.6

**Wiliam (87).** The chart is defensible as a rubric-assessed T2 LT, but it carries more delivery-condition assumptions than any other chart in the framework: specialist delivery from Band C, jurisdiction-specific Hungarian content at Bands D/E/F, and contested-claim curation at Band F. Assessment validity depends on whether those conditions are met in practice; the chart cannot guarantee that. The progression itself is sound — Band B to Band C (hygiene and reproductive anatomy) to Band D (risk assessment and protection methods) to Band E (healthcare navigation) to Band F (contested claims) is logical and evidence-anchored. But the rubric anchors at Band F ("evaluates at least two information sources against stated evidence-based guideline criteria" — Band E; "weighs the quality of evidence on each side" — Band F) become fair only if the unit-plan curation supplies comparable source material; otherwise the rubric floats. The chart would benefit from a Band-F exemplar task-set with pre-selected contested claims and paired evidence bases, built and reviewed as part of the programme guide, not left to each teacher.

**Claxton (88).** This LT is mostly scoped as performance and information literacy, which is the right call given the evidence types available — but there is a dispositional thread running through it (confidential help-seeking, honest risk-assessment, resisting ideological or commercial framings) that is handled implicitly rather than explicitly. At Band D the chart asks for "describing specific steps for accessing confidential health advice," which captures access-knowledge but not the underlying disposition of "I can ask for help about this without shame or fear"; at Band F the chart asks for a reasoned personal position on a contested claim, which is close to dispositional but framed as a written output. Not a defect — the LT is explicitly T2 — but worth noting in the authoring notes that the dispositional counterpart (treating SRH as a normal domain of self-care rather than a secret or shameful one) lives partly in LT 3.2 and LT 1.1 and should be taught alongside.

**Christodoulou (86).** The cognitive load across Bands D–F is heavier than any other chart in the framework. Band D packages STI (with four named examples), pregnancy risk, four protection-method categories, five substance-use categories, and confidential-access pathways — an honest count is closer to fifteen distinct knowledge elements in a single Band. Band E adds healthcare navigation, jurisdictional rights, three named evidence-based guideline organisations, and a taught evaluation method. Band F adds contested-claim content, commercial/institutional interests, ideological framings, and principles of critical appraisal. The chart is unit-plan-dependent in a way no other chart is: without explicit worked examples, spaced retrieval, and curated source material, the content risks being taught-and-forgotten rather than taught-and-usable. The authoring notes acknowledge this ("Band D substance-use framing should be harm-reduction"; "Band F contested-claim selection requires deliberate curation") but the acknowledgement cannot substitute for the unit plan.

**Koedinger (85).** Cognitive-load analysis: Band D KC count ≈ 15; Band E KC count ≈ 10; Band F KC count ≈ 8. Worked-example requirements scale non-linearly with KC count when the KCs are heterogeneous (STI facts, contraception mechanisms, Hungarian legal thresholds, source-evaluation criteria are four different knowledge types). The decomposition is broadly sensible but is asked to carry more than one band normally should. Recommended structural response: either split Band D into D-early (reproductive/puberty) and D-late (risk/protection), or pull the substance-use content out into a separate strand that cross-references this LT. Failing that, the unit plan must sequence D content across more instructional time than the other Band Ds in the framework, with explicit spaced-retrieval scheduling.

**Wineburg (87).** Band F contested-claim evaluation is in principle within my domain, but the execution depends entirely on which contested claims the unit plan supplies. Well-chosen examples (e.g. absolute vs relative risk of HPV-vaccine adverse events; the state of evidence on cannabis-mental-health association in adolescents) would make this an exemplary application of critical health literacy. Poorly chosen examples (one-sided or manufactured controversies) would collapse the task into a persuasion exercise. The chart rightly flags this as a unit-plan concern but does not specify curation principles — which sources count as "contested," how to select claims that are genuinely contested within the evidence base rather than politically contested outside it, and how to protect students against false-balance framings. A Band-F curation protocol belongs in the programme guide.

**Panel consensus recommendation for LT 4.2:** the chart is not structurally broken but is structurally dependent on unit-plan work that has not yet been done. Two specific unit-plan artefacts should be produced before Band D is taught in field use: (a) a Band-D sequencing plan splitting content into at least two instructional sub-units with spaced retrieval between them; (b) a Band-F contested-claim curation protocol naming at least three claims, paired evidence bases, and curation principles. Jurisdictional-fact verification (Hungarian legal thresholds, regulatory bodies) is a separate standing task the authoring notes already flag.

#### LT 7.1 Pattern Analysis & Adjustment — mean 87.8

**Wiliam (88).** The LT is rubric-friendly in principle — reflective tasks, metacognitive protocols, portfolio entries are all workable evidence formats. The structural concern is that every band's Know is "None standalone — lives in LT 6.1 Band X," which means the rubric's factual anchors are external and cumulative. If LT 6.1 coverage is incomplete or stale at the point of Band E's "structured metacognitive protocol," the Band E rubric loses its conceptual floor. The progression itself is sound (noticing → pattern → causal analysis → origin-vs-sustaining → structured protocol → personal framework) and the evidence formats are appropriate. The weakness is not the LT as written but the dependency on another LT's Know being reliably in place.

**Claxton (92).** This LT is pedagogically central to the framework. Pattern analysis and adjustment is exactly the learning-power move — noticing, analysing, intentionally adjusting. Band F's authored personal metacognitive framework is the right end-state. I do not share the structural concern about Know-cross-referencing: metacognitive work should build on the neuroscience rather than duplicate it, and the discipline of one Know-source-per-mechanism is good curriculum hygiene. This LT and LT 7.2 together carry the learning-power spine; I would resist any suggestion that the LT be strengthened by adding duplicative Know content.

**Christodoulou (84).** The LT is "None standalone. Know content lives in LT 6.1" at every band. For a T2 LT to have no standalone Know is structurally fragile: the LT's rubric anchors are parasitic on another LT's coverage, which means whenever LT 6.1 is taught partially (mid-entry students; teacher turnover; a compressed semester) this LT has nothing of its own to fall back on. In the criterion bank, the 49 `cross_lt_source_stated` edges added in REAL-4 include a high proportion on LT 7.1 — which confirms the structural dependency rather than resolving it. The right fix is not to duplicate LT 6.1 content but to give LT 7.1 its own metacognitive Know layer at each band (what a pattern is; what counts as a driver; what distinguishes origin from sustaining conditions; what a structured protocol is) — terminology and concepts that are specifically metacognitive rather than neuroscientific. The chart currently holds these in Understand, where they are harder to assess. Demote some Understand content to Know and the LT becomes assessable on its own terms.

**Koedinger (88).** Structurally the LT works as a KC sequence — pattern recognition, driver identification, origin-vs-sustaining distinction, protocol application, framework construction — and each KC is teachable. The concern is not the KC sequence but the coupling to LT 6.1. A cognitive tutor could track this sequence, but would need LT 6.1's KCs as first-class input variables. In the criterion bank the prerequisite structure captures this, but only through the soft-enabler and conceptual-accelerator edges, which by convention do not gate assessment. If the framework treats LT 6.1 Bands C–D as conceptual accelerators (not hard prerequisites) for LT 7.1, then a student can in principle land Band D on LT 7.1 without having Band D of LT 6.1 — which is cognitively implausible for the "distinguishing origin from sustaining conditions" move. Recommend making LT 6.1 Band C a hard prerequisite for LT 7.1 Band D, not a conceptual accelerator.

**Wineburg (87).** Outside my core domain, but I observe that the LT's evidence base (Flavell, Wilson, Zimmerman, Wood & Neal, Kegan) is reputable and well-matched to the bands. The practical concern is similar to what I see in civic-reasoning pedagogy: metacognition-as-generic-skill tends to produce confident talk without underlying capacity. The framework's intentional pairing with LT 7.2 (T3 dispositional counterpart) is a sound design answer. I would score higher if the LT had a named, disciplined protocol family at the Band E "structured metacognitive protocol" level — the chart describes the phases (mapping, sustaining-conditions identification, intervention, tracking, iteration) but does not name a protocol, which means teachers will need to instantiate one, with variable quality.

**Panel consensus recommendation for LT 7.1:** two structural edits should be considered before Band E is taught in field use: (a) move specific metacognitive terminology from Understand to Know at each band, so the LT has its own Know layer rather than relying entirely on LT 6.1; (b) reconsider the prerequisite typing to LT 6.1 — at minimum, LT 6.1 Band C should be a hard prerequisite for LT 7.1 Band D, not a conceptual accelerator. Christodoulou's and Koedinger's scores are the drivers of the flag; Claxton and Wineburg did not see the dependency structure as a defect. Resolving the flag does not require consensus on (a) and (b); the authoring notes should record the disagreement and the reason for whichever path is taken.

### Full commentary for flagged LT

#### LT 1.3 observation indicators — mean 87.8

**Wiliam (88).** The v2 Option 1 fix moves the Know load up (social identity theory and intersectionality from D to E), which eases the cognitive cliff but does not change the observation indicators themselves. The Band D Do — "identifying, with genuine reflection rather than surface performance, how at least one unchosen group membership shapes their own experience or perspective" — asks the observer to distinguish genuine reflection from surface performance, which is exactly the judgement the chart later admits is "correct but hard to operationalise" (authoring notes). The Band E Do — "voluntarily identifying how their cultural background might be limiting or distorting their interpretation… in contexts where this is not explicitly required" — is stronger because the "not explicitly required" qualifier is more observable than "genuine reflection." Band F Do ("engaging with perspectives that challenge their own identity framework… without dismissing them or retreating to a fixed position") is specific but asks for a lot: the observer must see engagement, absence of dismissal, absence of retreat, and absence of projection onto peers. That is four observations at once for a single band. An exemplar-case library for Band D and Band F would reduce the inter-observer variance materially.

**Claxton (90).** Identity-as-disposition is genuinely dispositional and these indicators are broadly the right kind of indicators. The Band D "genuine reflection" concern is softer for me than for others on the panel, because in practice trained observers working over weeks rather than a single lesson can tell the difference between a student who holds the move lightly and one who is saying the right words. The cross-context requirement is doing the work. My one concern is Band F indirection ("without projecting their own resolution process onto peers navigating different questions") — this is a real observation but it is a third-order one (observing what the student does not do in the presence of peers whose identity process is different), which in practice requires observer continuity across multiple peer interactions. Viable, but observer-time-intensive.

**Christodoulou (85).** This is the scoring driver. "Genuine reflection rather than surface performance" at Band D is the exact kind of indicator I consistently argue against: it asks the observer to judge authenticity, which is not falsifiable. A confident student can say the right things and be scored surface; a quieter student can say little and be scored reflective. The judgement track is invisible and becomes a site of observer bias. The Band E Do is slightly better because "not explicitly required" gives the observer an external condition to check ("was this prompted?") rather than an internal state to judge ("was this genuine?"). The Band F Do stacks four observations; in practice any assessment protocol that asks for four simultaneous observations on a single indicator tends to reduce to whichever one the observer finds easiest. Recommend: (a) replace "genuine reflection rather than surface performance" at Band D with a behavioural anchor — e.g. "the student names a specific unchosen group membership and gives a specific example of how it shapes an experience, without the example being a repetition of a class discussion example"; (b) consolidate Band F into two observations rather than four, with the remaining two logged as conditions-present rather than positive indicators.

**Koedinger (87).** Developmental graduation across A–F is real, not vocabulary escalation — A names differences without valuing, B names multiple dimensions, C pauses to consider one's own lens, D names an unchosen membership, E identifies lens-distortion under no external requirement, F engages-without-retreating. That sequence is cognitively coherent. The indicator design weakness is concentrated at Band D and Band F, which are the bands where the observer is asked to make an authenticity judgement. An operationalising protocol — with exemplar responses at each band, anchor cases for "genuine reflection" vs "surface performance," and inter-observer reliability checks — would move this LT up meaningfully. The criterion bank's cross_lt_source_stated edges already link D and E to LT 6.1 Knows, which is appropriate; what is missing is an observation-indicator exemplar library.

**Wineburg (89).** Identity work is within my broader civic-literacy concern. The indicators do not, on the face of it, over-promise — they name observable student moves (naming a difference without valuing it, naming more than one identity dimension, pausing to consider interpretive lens, identifying unchosen membership, voluntarily identifying lens-distortion, engaging with challenge without retreating). The Band D "genuine vs performance" concern is real but it is a general concern about identity assessment rather than specific to this framework; most identity-work assessments hit it. The practical question is whether the observation protocol (which does not live in the chart) is good enough to handle it. My score reflects that the chart itself is reasonable; the observation protocol is where the work is.

**Panel consensus recommendation for LT 1.3:** two operational steps are needed before Band D and Band F are used for assessment in field conditions: (a) a behavioural anchor replacement for "genuine reflection rather than surface performance" (Christodoulou's specific suggested wording, or equivalent); (b) an observation-indicator exemplar library with anchor cases at each band. Neither requires chart revision; both belong in the observation protocol documentation for T3 LTs. This is the same class of observation-infrastructure concern flagged for LT 1.1 Band F and LT 3.2 Band D in the v2 fix session (out-of-scope list); LT 1.3 should be added to that list for the same reason.
