# Centre-of-gravity briefs — RSHE, Welsh CfW, CASEL

**Purpose.** Capture each external framework on its own terms before any comparative writing. Every claim resolves to a paragraph in `sources/`. Used to validate, before drafting Level 1, that each framework has been read fairly. Not a comparative document.

**Structure.** Each brief has three parts:
1. **In their own words** — ~100-word paraphrase using only the framework's own vocabulary.
2. **What it foregrounds** — three sentences, each tied to a paragraph citation.
3. **What it deliberately doesn't do** — the framework's own statement of scope limits, where one exists. May be short or empty if the source doesn't supply this directly.

## RSHE — UK Department for Education statutory guidance, July 2025

### In their own words

RSHE is statutory guidance on Relationships Education, Relationships and Sex Education and Health Education. Its premise is that children and young people need knowledge and skills to make informed and ethical decisions about their wellbeing, health and relationships, and that high-quality, evidence-based teaching can prepare them for the opportunities and responsibilities of adult life and prevent harms. The guidance specifies what schools should cover by the end of primary and by the end of secondary, sets out seven guiding principles for how teaching should be designed and delivered, and gives parents a right to request withdrawal from sex education within defined limits.

### What it foregrounds

The guidance opens by naming knowledge and skills, alongside the cultivation of "positive characteristics including resilience, self-worth, self-respect, honesty, integrity, courage, kindness, and trustworthiness," as the core of effective teaching, and by tying effective teaching directly to harm prevention — helping young people "understand and identify when things are not right" [rshe-foreword §1]. Curriculum content is specified as terminal goals — what pupils should know by the end of primary and the end of secondary — with within-phase sequencing left to schools [rshe-foreword §3, §15d]. The role of parents is structurally embedded: schools must consult parents on policy, all materials should be available to parents, and parents may request withdrawal from sex education up to three terms before a pupil turns sixteen [rshe-foreword §12, §15b, §16, §19].

### What it deliberately doesn't do

The guidance is explicit about its boundaries. Relationships education "doesn't involve explaining the detail of different forms of sexual activity" even when it covers sensitive topics such as sexual violence [rshe-foreword §14]. Effective RSE "does not encourage or normalise early sexual experimentation" [rshe-strand-introductions §33]. Schools "should avoid language which might normalise harmful behaviour among young people — for example gendered language which might normalise male violence or stigmatise boys" [rshe-foreword §15c]. Sex education at primary is non-statutory; the guidance recommends it be taught in years 5 or 6 if at all [rshe-strand-introductions §30]. Parents' right of withdrawal from sex education is itself a deliberate structural limit on the framework's reach.

## Welsh CfW — Curriculum for Wales, Health and Well-being Area of Learning and Experience

### In their own words

The Health and Well-being Area of Learning and Experience is one of the Areas within the Curriculum for Wales, all of which support learners to develop towards four overarching purposes: ambitious capable learners; enterprising creative contributors; ethical informed citizens; healthy confident individuals. Within Health and Well-being, five "What Matters" statements act as the substantive frame: physical health and well-being has lifelong benefits; how we process and respond to our experiences affects mental health and emotional well-being; our decision-making impacts our lives and others' lives; how we engage with social influences shapes who we are; healthy relationships are fundamental to our well-being. Each is a claim the curriculum is built around. Progression runs through Progression Steps that the framework treats as reference waypoints rather than year-grouped checklists.

### What it foregrounds

The Area is framed as a curatorial enabler rather than a content prescriber: each "What Matters" passage opens with "This Area can help learners to…" rather than "pupils should know" [cfw-what-matters §3, §7, §11, §15, §18]. Progression is described as deepening rather than accumulation — "each phase represents the building blocks of progression… consolidating and strengthening the same dispositions, knowledge and skills and applying them in new and relevant contexts as they develop. This is very different to simply acquiring learning about topics in isolation and then moving on to other content" [cfw-rse-statutory-principles §11]. Relationships and sexuality education is positioned as a cross-cutting theme that runs through the curriculum and "encompasses the knowledge, skills, dispositions and values" that empower learners [cfw-rse-statutory-principles §5].

### What it deliberately doesn't do

The framework explicitly distances itself from topic-acquisition models of progression — phase movement is "very different to simply acquiring learning about topics in isolation and then moving on to other content" [cfw-rse-statutory-principles §11]. Schools should "include learners' perspectives" and "provide increasing opportunities for learners to help direct their learning as they progress" [cfw-rse-statutory-principles §9], a deliberate departure from teacher-specified content lists. Within the philosophical material captured here, the framework does not list further self-imposed limits.

## CASEL — Collaborative for Academic, Social, and Emotional Learning

### In their own words

CASEL describes social and emotional learning as the process by which young people and adults acquire and apply the knowledge, skills, and attitudes needed to develop healthy identities, manage emotions, achieve personal and collective goals, feel and show empathy, establish and maintain supportive relationships, and make responsible and caring decisions. The framework — known as the CASEL wheel — has two parts: five interrelated competencies (Self-Awareness, Self-Management, Social Awareness, Relationship Skills, Responsible Decision-Making), and four nested settings (Classrooms, Schools, Families and Caregivers, Communities) in which competencies are practised. Each competency is defined as "the abilities to…" do something; SEL is positioned as a process and a practice across the lifespan from Pre-K through adulthood.

### What it foregrounds

Each of the five competencies is defined in terms of action — "abilities," "capacities" — rather than knowledge to be retained: Self-Awareness is "the abilities to understand one's own emotions, thoughts, and values and how they influence behavior across contexts" [casel-framework-definition §5]; Relationship Skills is "the abilities to establish and maintain healthy and supportive relationships and to effectively navigate settings with diverse individuals and groups" [casel-framework-definition §8]. Competence is positioned as inseparable from the contexts in which it is practised — the framework's four settings rings (Classrooms, Schools, Families & Caregivers, Communities) make environments part of the framework, not a backdrop to it [casel-framework-definition §10–§13]. Equity is structural rather than thematic: "transformative SEL" applies the framework toward "creating equitable settings and systems and promoting justice-oriented school and civic engagement," and adult bias-awareness is named as a precondition for equitable SEL [casel-equity-transformative-sel §1, §4].

### What it deliberately doesn't do

The CASEL materials captured here do not name the framework's deliberate limits in the way RSHE does. CASEL presents itself as universal in scope (Pre-K through adults) and broadly applicable across schools, districts and states. Honest finding: the source corpus does not supply a CASEL self-statement of "what this framework is not for."
