# LT 8.2 — Digital Influence & Psychological Agency

REAL learning target description: identify how digital products shape attention and behaviour, analyse personal effects, and justify reasoned responses to persuasive design systems. Bands A–F.

---

## Comparison with RSHE

RSHE's secondary online-safety content addresses persuasive design directly — "addictive design" is named within online gaming risks, alongside "data and consent rights" and how "search-engine information selection and targeting" shape what users see [rshe-pilot-extracts §21]. REAL's LT 8.2 develops these mechanisms across bands: persuasive-design vocabulary at Band C, the attention-economy business model and recommendation algorithms at Band E, and a personal research-anchored position by the end [real-framework-overview §68, §69, §70].

---

## Comparison with Welsh CfW

Digital technology appears in CfW's Purpose 1 in productive terms: learners "use digital technologies creatively to communicate, find and analyse information" [cfw-four-purposes §4]. Persuasive design — the way digital products are engineered to capture attention and shape behaviour — sits outside that framing. REAL's LT 8.2 specifies it at vocabulary level (variable reward, intermittent reinforcement, dark patterns) and at Band F evaluates the cumulative effect against research evidence [real-framework-overview §68, §69, §70].

---

## Comparison with CASEL

CASEL addresses digital media within Relationship Skills: at G6–8, students "demonstrate positive communication skills, including interactions on social and digital media, and reflect on the potential impact on their reputation and relationships" [casel-pilot-extracts §24]. The framing is interpersonal — what the student says and how it lands. REAL's LT 8.2 sits on the other side of the screen: how the platform is engineered to shape attention, emotion, and behaviour, and what to do about it given current research [real-framework-overview §68, §69, §70].
