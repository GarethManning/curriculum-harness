# Theme 8 — Equity and the social surround

How RSHE, Welsh CfW, CASEL, and REAL each treat equity and the social-surround territory that REAL holds across LTs 4.3, 5.2, 1.3, and 4.1.

---

Equity content shows up across all four frameworks, but at different structural levels. The question this theme asks is where each framework places equity — at the architectural top, or distributed across competencies and learning targets. In REAL the territory spans four learning targets: anti-discrimination and bystander analysis (LT 4.3), community engagement and civic purpose (LT 5.2), cultural-self-awareness and unchosen group memberships (LT 1.3), and consent including power dynamics (LT 4.1).

RSHE addresses prejudice and equality through specific content. At secondary, pupils learn "how stereotypes, in particular stereotypes based on sex, gender reassignment, race, religion, sexual orientation or disability, can cause damage" and "should be equipped to recognise misogyny and other forms of prejudice" [rshe-pilot-extracts §35]. Effective teaching cultivates "positive characteristics including resilience, self-worth, self-respect, honesty, integrity, courage, kindness, and trustworthiness" [rshe-foreword §1]. The framing is curriculum-content-on-prejudice plus character-outcomes; equity is not foregrounded as a structural framing of the whole programme.

Welsh CfW treats the social surround as one of its What Matters statements: WM4 — "How we engage with social influences shapes who we are and affects our health and well-being" [cfw-what-matters §14]. Purpose 3 commits learners to becoming "ethical, informed citizens who… respect the needs and rights of others, as a member of a diverse society" [cfw-four-purposes §6]. The RSE Code lists "promote equality and equity of sex, gender and sexuality" among the protective outcomes of high-quality RSE [cfw-rse-statutory-principles §4]. Equity is distributed across multiple framework elements — Purpose 3, WM4, and the RSE Code — without being declared as the framework's structural orientation.

CASEL elevates equity to framework level through Transformative SEL: "Transformative SEL is a term that refers to applying the SEL framework toward the goals of creating equitable settings and systems and promoting justice-oriented school and civic engagement" [casel-equity-transformative-sel §1]. At Grade 9–10, students "recognise and describe unfairness and injustice in many forms including attitudes, speech, behaviors, policies, practices, and laws" [casel-pilot-extracts §26]. Adult practice is also named — equity-conscious teaching requires "awareness of students' cultures and backgrounds" and willingness to "challenge any inequitable practices" [casel-equity-transformative-sel §4]. Equity is the framework's structural orientation, not a distributed thread.

REAL distributes the territory across four learning targets without giving it framework-level status. LT 4.3 (Active Bystander & Anti-Discrimination) reaches Band F with the student analysing a real bystander success or failure and proposing "specific group, school, or community-level conditions that would change the outcome next time" [real-framework-overview §47]. LT 5.2 (Community Engagement & Purpose) reaches Band E with "a sustained initiative (beyond a single project cycle) addressing a genuine need, navigating specific institutional and relational obstacles as they arise" [real-framework-overview §55]. LT 1.3 develops cultural self-awareness including unchosen group memberships at Band D [real-framework-overview §28]. LT 4.1 names power-dynamic content directly at Band D: "Power imbalance occurs when one person has more age, status, role authority, peer-group standing, or control over outcomes than the other" [real-framework-overview §43]. The territory is substantively present; it is not foregrounded as a framework-level orientation in the way Transformative SEL frames CASEL.

The four frameworks all contain equity content; the structural question is where each places it. RSHE specifies prejudice-and-stereotype content as curriculum topics, with character outcomes layered alongside. CfW distributes equity across Purpose 3, WM4, and the RSE Code's protective outcomes. CASEL elevates equity to framework-level through Transformative SEL. REAL builds the territory across four learning targets at the LT level. The structural difference is real, and the comparison surfaces it most clearly here. Whether the structural placement matters for a given school depends on what equity-related work the school wants its framework to do upfront, and what it is comfortable building case by case across the LTs.
