# How REAL's approach compares to RSHE, Welsh CfW, and CASEL

Three other wellbeing curricula sit alongside REAL's: the UK's statutory Relationships, Sex and Health Education guidance; the Welsh Government's Health and Well-being Area of Learning and Experience; and the CASEL framework for social and emotional learning. They look superficially similar — they all teach children about emotions, relationships, health and decisions. But each is doing something different, organised around different premises, ending up specifying different things.

**RSHE.** The UK statutory guidance is, on its own terms, a national specification of what every child should know. It begins from the premise that "children and young people need knowledge and skills that will enable them to make informed and ethical decisions about their wellbeing, health and relationships" [rshe-foreword §1]. Content is specified as terminal goals — what pupils should cover by the end of primary, and by the end of secondary — within a curriculum that schools themselves sequence and design [rshe-foreword §3, §15d]. Parental engagement is structural: schools must engage parents in policy, all materials must be available to them, and parents may request withdrawal from sex education within defined limits [rshe-foreword §12, §16]. RSHE is a statutory minimum delivered within a national education system that already specifies subject knowledge.

**Welsh CfW.** The Health and Well-being Area takes a different shape. Relationships and sexuality education "encompasses the knowledge, skills, dispositions and values that will empower learners" — not a content checklist [cfw-rse-statutory-principles §5]. Most distinctively, progression is defined as deepening, not topic-acquisition: each phase represents "the building blocks of progression… consolidating and strengthening the same dispositions, knowledge and skills and applying them in new and relevant contexts as they develop. This is very different to simply acquiring learning about topics in isolation and then moving on to other content" [cfw-rse-statutory-principles §11]. Schools are expected to "provide increasing opportunities for learners to help direct their learning as they progress" [cfw-rse-statutory-principles §9]. The result is a framework of principles plus learner agency rather than specified content.

**CASEL.** CASEL defines social and emotional learning as "the process through which all young people and adults acquire and apply the knowledge, skills, and attitudes" needed to develop healthy identities, manage emotions, build relationships and make caring decisions [casel-framework-definition §1]. Its five competencies — Self-Awareness, Self-Management, Social Awareness, Relationship Skills, Responsible Decision-Making — are each defined as abilities to do something, not knowledge to retain. Self-Awareness is "the abilities to understand one's own emotions, thoughts, and values and how they influence behavior across contexts" [casel-framework-definition §5]. The framework wraps the five competencies in four nested settings — classrooms, schools, families and caregivers, communities — making environments part of the framework rather than a backdrop [casel-framework-definition §10–§13]. "Transformative SEL" extends the framework toward equity and justice-oriented school engagement [casel-equity-transformative-sel §1].

**REAL.** A teacher might notice a student "naming a recurring personal trigger — time of day, type of task, particular social configuration — and selecting a strategy from their practised repertoire before the trigger escalates, across more than one setting" [real-framework-overview §5]. That kind of moment — repeated across contexts, over time — is what REAL's framework is built around. Each learning target specifies what students should know, understand and do; for targets that develop a disposition, the most distinctive part is what students do. Progress is recorded through observation indicators beginning "the teacher notices the student…" — patterns of action across many situations, not single correct answers or assessed pieces of work [real-framework-overview §9]. A disposition is established when it has become part of who the student is at this stage.

**What the comparison reveals.** Each framework is internally coherent on its own terms. RSHE specifies a statutory minimum of knowledge and skills by phase. Welsh CfW frames progression as deepening competence through revisited contexts. CASEL frames competencies as abilities practised across nested settings, under an equity-oriented vision of human development. REAL's shape — practised disposition becoming part of who the student is — has different costs and benefits to all three. This is one reading of how the frameworks compare.
